辨茶之道

A discerning palate is cultivated over time. Here are the key signs that separate exceptional tea from ordinary.

好茶 Good Tea

Appearance: Leaves are uniform, intact, with a natural sheen. Colours are vibrant — deep greens, silver-tipped, rich amber.

Aroma: Clean, layered fragrance that transforms — floral to woody to honey as it cools.

Liquor: Clear, bright, and jewel-toned. No cloudiness or sediment.

Taste: A long finish — sweetness lingers in the throat (回甘 huí gān) long after swallowing.

Wet leaves: Elastic, supple, and whole. Leaves revive beautifully after multiple steepings.

劣茶 Poor Tea

Appearance: Broken, dusty, or uneven. Unnatural colour from over-processing or additives.

Aroma: Flat, musty, or artificially perfumed. The scent disappears quickly.

Liquor: Cloudy, dull, or muddy. Often overly dark from poor processing or mould.

Taste: Harsh bitterness that does not transform. Dry, astringent aftertaste with no sweetness.

Wet leaves: Brittle, fragmented, or slimy. Fades after one or two steepings.

茶師的秘訣 — The Master's Tip

The truest test of any tea is its third and fourth steeping. Good tea reveals deeper layers with each infusion. Poor tea collapses quickly, leaving nothing but a watery ghost of its first cup.

老茶之道
The Way of Aged Tea

Old tea — particularly aged pu-erh and white tea — has become the focus of serious collectors and connoisseurs. This is not mere fashion; it is rooted in science, tradition, and the pursuit of complexity that only time can create.

Why Time Matters
Live microbial cultures within compressed pu-erh continue to transform the tea over decades. Tannins mellow, bitterness fades, and entirely new aromatic compounds emerge — camphor, dates, dried fruit, aged wood.
Rarity & Provenance
As forests of ancient tea trees (古樹 gǔ shù) shrink and old-growth material becomes scarcer, teas made from century-old trees command extraordinary respect. The tree's age imparts a mineral depth and "throaty" body impossible to replicate in younger plants.
Health & Gentleness
Aged tea is gentler on the stomach. The harsh compounds that make young raw pu-erh challenging are transformed over time. Many people who cannot drink fresh tea find old tea smooth and soothing.
The Return to Roots
Modern tea culture is returning to old tea as a corrective to the fast-consumption era. At 斗茗居, we believe each cup of old tea is a dialogue across time — between the farmer who pressed it, the years that transformed it, and you who drink it now.
